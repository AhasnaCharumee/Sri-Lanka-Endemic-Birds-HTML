Endemic Birds in Sri Lanka

Legge’s Flowerpecker - ශ්‍රී ලංකා පිළිලිච්චා

The Legge’s Flowerpecker (Dicaeum vincens) is a small yet vibrant bird species endemic to Sri Lanka, primarily found in the island's wet zone, dry zone forests, and home gardens with dense vegetation. Named after the British ornithologist William Vincent Legge, this bird belongs to the Dicaeidae family, commonly known as flowerpeckers, which are tiny, nectar-feeding birds playing a vital role in forest ecosystems through pollination and seed dispersal. Measuring about 8 cm in length, Legge’s Flowerpecker is one of the smallest birds in Sri Lanka, often mistaken for a flying insect due to its swift, darting movements among flowers. Its plumage is predominantly dark olive-green on the upper parts, while its underparts are a pale yellowish or buff-white color. A distinct whitish throat and belly, combined with a relatively short tail and a slightly curved beak, help differentiate it from other small passerine birds. The beak is specially adapted for feeding on nectar, fruits, and small insects, making it an essential pollinator in the Sri Lankan ecosystem. This species is particularly fond of mistletoe plants, as they provide both food and nesting sites.

Despite its tiny size, Legge’s Flowerpecker is highly active and agile, constantly flitting between flowers in search of nectar and small berries. It plays a crucial role in dispersing mistletoe seeds, which attach themselves to tree branches and grow into parasitic plants. This mutualistic relationship benefits both the flowerpecker and the plant species it feeds on, as the bird spreads seeds across the forest while obtaining sustenance. Besides nectar and fruits, this bird also consumes small insects, spiders, and caterpillars, particularly during the breeding season when protein-rich food is needed to feed its young. Its high-pitched, sharp “tsip-tsip” calls are often the first indication of its presence, as it swiftly moves through the tree canopy.

The breeding season of Legge’s Flowerpecker typically occurs between February and May, though some nesting activities may extend into August. The female builds a small, oval-shaped nest, often suspended from the underside of a broad leaf or hidden within dense foliage. The nest is intricately woven from plant fibers, cobwebs, and soft materials, providing a secure shelter for the two to three eggs laid inside. Both parents share feeding duties, ensuring that the chicks receive a steady supply of regurgitated fruits and insects until they are ready to fledge.

Although Legge’s Flowerpecker is relatively common in Sri Lanka’s forested areas, its population faces threats due to deforestation, habitat destruction, and urbanization. The increasing loss of natural forest cover and the decline of native mistletoe plants have impacted its food sources and nesting sites. However, due to its adaptability, this species has also been observed in home gardens, rubber plantations, and secondary forests, indicating some level of resilience to environmental changes. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) currently lists Legge’s Flowerpecker as a species of Least Concern (LC), but conservation efforts are essential to protect its natural habitats from further degradation.

As an important pollinator and seed disperser, Legge’s Flowerpecker plays a vital ecological role in maintaining the health of Sri Lanka’s forests. Conservation efforts, including reforestation projects, awareness campaigns, and the protection of native plant species, are crucial to ensuring the survival of this tiny but significant bird. For birdwatchers and nature enthusiasts, spotting Legge’s Flowerpecker can be a rewarding experience, especially in places like Sinharaja Forest Reserve, Horton Plains, Kitulgala, and lowland rainforest regions, where its presence is most prominent. In conclusion, this charming, energetic, and ecologically important bird symbolizes the intricate balance of Sri Lanka’s avian biodiversity, highlighting the need for continued conservation and awareness efforts to protect its habitat for generations to come.
