Endemic Birds in Sri Lanka

Red-faced Malkoha-ශ්‍රි ලංකා වත රතු මල්කොහා

The Red-faced Malkoha (Phaenicophaeus pyrrhocephalus) is a striking and elusive bird species endemic to Sri Lanka, primarily found in the dense forests of the wet zone and lowland rainforests. This bird belongs to the cuckoo family (Cuculidae) but differs from typical cuckoos in its non-parasitic breeding habits. Its most distinctive feature is its bright red facial skin, which contrasts beautifully with its dark greenish-black plumage, long tail, and robust, slightly curved beak. This bird measures around 40 to 50 cm in length, with a long, graduated tail that aids in balance as it moves through the forest canopy. The iridescent green and bluish-black feathers on its back and wings make it blend seamlessly with the foliage, providing excellent camouflage against predators. The Red-faced Malkoha is primarily arboreal, meaning it spends most of its life in trees, skillfully navigating branches with its strong claws and long tail. Unlike many other cuckoo species, it does not engage in brood parasitism, meaning it does not lay its eggs in the nests of other birds; instead, it builds its own nest and raises its young.

This species thrives in primary and secondary forests, particularly in the rainforests of Sinharaja Forest Reserve, Kanneliya, Kitulgala, and the lower elevations of the Knuckles Mountain Range. It prefers habitats with dense undergrowth and tall trees, where it can forage for food safely away from predators. Although it occasionally ventures into well-wooded home gardens and rubber plantations, it is rarely seen in open areas or urban environments. The Red-faced Malkoha is an omnivore, with a diet consisting of insects, small reptiles, frogs, fruits, and berries. It is particularly fond of caterpillars, beetles, grasshoppers, and spiders, which it plucks from leaves and tree bark using its sharp beak. Additionally, it plays a crucial ecological role in seed dispersal by consuming various fruits and berries, later excreting the seeds in different locations, thereby aiding in forest regeneration. This feeding behavior highlights its importance in maintaining a healthy ecosystem. Observing the Red-faced Malkoha in the wild requires patience, as it is known for its shy and secretive nature. Unlike noisy and conspicuous birds, it moves quietly through dense vegetation, relying on its camouflage to avoid detection. However, its presence is often revealed by its soft, low-pitched "kok-kok" or "krow-krow" calls, which it uses to communicate with its mate or signal alarm when a predator is nearby.

During the breeding season, which typically occurs between March and July, this species constructs a cup-shaped nest hidden within dense foliage, often placed high in the trees to protect it from ground predators. The nest is built using twigs, leaves, and moss, and both parents share the responsibility of incubating the eggs and feeding the hatchlings. The clutch usually consists of two to three eggs, which hatch after an incubation period of about 14 to 16 days. The young birds remain in the nest for a few weeks before fledging, during which time the parents tirelessly provide food and protection. Due to its restricted range and dependence on forest habitats, the Red-faced Malkoha faces several threats, including deforestation, habitat fragmentation, and human disturbances. As Sri Lanka continues to experience rapid urbanization and agricultural expansion, many lowland rainforests have been cleared for tea, rubber, and palm oil plantations, reducing the available habitat for this bird. Additionally, illegal logging and forest encroachment pose significant threats, making conservation efforts critical for its survival.

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) has listed the Red-faced Malkoha as Near Threatened (NT), indicating that while it is not yet endangered, its population is declining and requires proactive conservation measures. To protect this species, several initiatives have been implemented, including forest conservation programs, eco-tourism promotion, and reforestation projects. The Sinharaja Forest Reserve, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, plays a vital role in safeguarding the remaining populations of the Red-faced Malkoha, providing a protected environment where it can thrive without human interference. Moreover, organizations such as the Sri Lanka Wildlife Conservation Society (SLWCS) and the Department of Wildlife Conservation actively work to enforce environmental laws and raise awareness about the importance of preserving native bird species. Birdwatchers and nature enthusiasts visiting Sri Lanka often seek out the Red-faced Malkoha due to its rarity and unique appearance. The best chance of spotting one is in the early morning or late afternoon, when it is most active. A good pair of binoculars, patience, and a keen ear for its soft calls are essential for successful observation. As an integral part of Sri Lanka’s biodiversity, this bird represents the rich avian heritage of the island and highlights the importance of preserving its natural habitats.

While conservation efforts have helped maintain stable populations in protected areas, continued deforestation and climate change remain ongoing threats. Encouraging sustainable forestry practices, promoting eco-tourism, and increasing awareness among local communities about the ecological role of the Red-faced Malkoha can significantly contribute to its long-term survival. In conclusion, the Red-faced Malkoha is a fascinating and beautiful bird that embodies the rich biodiversity of Sri Lanka’s rainforests. Its distinctive red facial markings, long tail, secretive nature, and omnivorous diet make it a key player in the ecosystem, aiding in both insect population control and seed dispersal. However, due to habitat loss and human activities, this species is now facing increasing threats, emphasizing the need for conservation efforts. By protecting its natural habitat and supporting conservation programs, we can ensure that future generations will continue to admire and appreciate the beauty and ecological importance of the Red-faced Malkoha in Sri Lanka’s forests.
