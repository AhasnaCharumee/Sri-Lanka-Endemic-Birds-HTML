Endemic Birds in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka Crested Drongo-ශ්‍රී ලංකා සිළු කවුඩා


About 30 cm, this glossy black bird is common in the remaining low country wet zone forests. The prominent crest and long tail feathers separate them from the other Drongos in the area. They are a “nuclear” member of the mixed species feeding flocks of the low country wet zone. Playing the role of a “sentinel” species, it is often heard through the forest canopy.

The nest is built on an open branch attached to it with mosses and lichens. The shallow cup holds around 2-3 eggs. The hatchlings stay stiff in the nest well camouflaged, even though the nest is exposed. The parent birds defend the nest and nestlings very well.

The different shape and position of the wattles and the stouter orange-red bill distinguish this species from the southern hill myna, which also occurs in Sri Lankan forests. The sexes are similar in plumage, but can be distinguished by iris color, which is pale in females and dark in males. Juveniles have a duller bill and smaller wattles, and are less glossy overall.

This species is highly active and often follows groups of monkeys or other animals, taking advantage of the insects that get disturbed by their movement. The Sri Lanka Crested Drongo is also known for its aggressive behavior when defending its territory, often chasing away much larger birds. Its diet mainly consists of insects, small reptiles, and even nectar from certain flowers. With its striking red eyes, this bird stands out against the green forest backdrop. It uses its strong beak to catch flying insects mid-air, showcasing its impressive hunting skills. The species plays a crucial role in the ecosystem by controlling insect populations and contributing to seed dispersal. Unfortunately, deforestation and habitat destruction pose a threat to the Sri Lanka Crested Drongo. As a forest-dependent species, the loss of natural habitats can significantly impact its survival. Conservation efforts are necessary to preserve Sri Lanka’s biodiversity and ensure that unique species like the Crested Drongo continue to thrive in their natural environment.
