Endemic Birds in Sri Lanka

Ashy Headed Laughing Thrush - හිස අළු දෙමලිච්චා


The Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush is a rare and fascinating bird species endemic to Sri Lanka. It belongs to the Leiothrichidae family and is well known for its sociable nature and unique laughing calls. This species is mainly found in lowland and submontane rainforests, where it thrives in dense undergrowth and bamboo thickets. It is often seen in small flocks, moving actively through the foliage while foraging for food.

The Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush is a medium-sized bird, measuring approximately 22–25 cm in length. It has a distinct ashy-gray head, which contrasts with its olive-brown upperparts and paler underparts. A dark eye-stripe runs from the base of its curved, strong beak to the sides of its head, giving it a striking appearance. Its long, slightly graduated tail helps it maintain balance while hopping between branches..

The Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush is omnivorous, feeding on a variety of insects, small fruits, berries, and seeds. It often forages on the forest floor, flipping leaves and twigs to find hidden prey. Occasionally, it follows mixed-species feeding flocks, joining other birds in search of food. This species is primarily found in the southwestern and central regions of Sri Lanka, including well-known rainforest reserves such as:

  • Sinharaja Forest Reserve
  • Kitulgala Rainforest
  • Morapitiya Forest
  • Kanneliya Forest Reserve

The Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush prefers dense forests with thick undergrowth, often near water sources. It thrives in humid environments and is less commonly seen in secondary forests or human-modified landscapes.

This bird is highly social, moving in groups of 6–10 individuals. It is extremely vocal, producing a variety of chattering, whistling, and laughing-like calls, which help the flock communicate while foraging. The bird's call is one of the most recognizable sounds in Sri Lanka’s rainforests. Although it is an active flier, the Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush prefers to move by hopping between branches and undergrowth. It is often seen playing, preening each other, and engaging in cooperative behaviors within its flock.

Due to deforestation, habitat fragmentation, and climate change, the Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush is classified as Near Threatened (NT) on the IUCN Red List. The biggest threat to its survival is the destruction of Sri Lanka’s rainforests for agriculture, logging, and human settlements. Several conservation programs focus on protecting Sinharaja and other rainforests, which are crucial for the survival of this species. Strengthening these conservation efforts is essential to ensure that the Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush continues to thrive in the wild.

The Ashy-Headed Laughingthrush is an important symbol of Sri Lanka’s rich avian biodiversity. Its fascinating social behavior, unique calls, and dependence on dense rainforests make it a treasured species among birdwatchers and conservationists. Preserving its natural habitat is critical for ensuring that future generations can continue to admire this remarkable bird.
