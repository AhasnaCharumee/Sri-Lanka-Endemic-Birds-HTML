Introduction

Sri Lanka is a tropical paradise blessed with an extraordinary variety of endemic bird species that are found nowhere else in the world. These birds play a vital role in maintaining ecological balance and are an essential part of our natural heritage. However, due to increasing human activities such as deforestation, pollution, and climate change, many of these species are now under serious threat. OUR PROJECTS

Horton Plains Biodiversity Project

Protects cloud forest ecosystem. Home to Sri Lanka Bush Warbler, Yellow-eared Bulbul. Supported by Department of Wildlife Conservation.

Sinharaja Forest Reserve Conservation

UNESCO World Heritage Site. Home to endemic species like Sri Lanka Blue Magpie. Promotes biodiversity and eco-tourism.

Kanneliya Forest Bird Survey Projects

University of Colombo Main focus: Endemic birds study, research, school awareness programs. Popular programs: Bird ringing, monthly birdwatching events.

FOGSL (Field Ornithology Group of Sri Lanka)

UNESCO World Heritage Site. Home to endemic species like Sri Lanka Blue Magpie. Promotes biodiversity and eco-tourism.

Success Stories

The Comeback of the Sri Lanka Blue Magpie

Sri Lanka Blue Magpie was once threatened due to habitat loss in Sinharaja. Thanks to conservation by FOGSL and the Forest Department, its population is now recovering

Saving the Sri Lanka Whistling Thrush

The Sri Lanka Whistling Thrush (Arrenga) is found only in highland forests like Horton Plains. Once listed as endangered, its numbers are improving thanks to monitoring and restricted access to nesting areas.

The Rise of Bird Tourism in Kitulgala

Birdwatching tourism has grown in Kitulgala, bringing income to local communities while protecting species like the Chestnut-backed Owlet and Green-billed Coucal.

Crackdown on Illegal Bird Trade

Thanks to citizen reporting and stricter enforcement, several rare endemic birds were rescued from the illegal pet trade in recent years.
