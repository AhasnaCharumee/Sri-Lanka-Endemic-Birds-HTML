Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler-ශ්‍රි ලංකා දෑකැති දෙමළිච්චා


The males and females look alike and measure around 22 cm. The long sickle shaped yellow bill, the habit of feeding by searching on the trunk of trees and the very characteristic call enable the birds to be identified easily.This species is primarily found in the montane and submontane forests of Sri Lanka, typically at higher elevations in the island’s central and southwestern regions. The Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler prefers dense, shady forests, where it thrives in areas with thick undergrowth. Key locations where this bird can be spotted include Horton Plains, Sinharaja Forest Reserve, and Knuckles Mountain Range.

The bird is found in all suitable humid tall forest patches in the island except in the north peninsular. Its present distribution is primarily restricted to this habitat preference. In the hill country the bird is seen in the estates with good tree cover, specially along river reservations.The Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler is primarily an insectivorous bird, foraging on the forest floor for insects, small invertebrates, and other arthropods. It uses its long, curved bill to probe through leaf litter and undergrowth in search of food. This bird is highly energetic and often travels in small groups, actively moving through dense vegetation. The Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler is known for its loud and complex calls, which are used for communication within its group. These calls can be heard echoing through the forests during the early morning and late evening.

They are regular members of mixed species feeding flocks of the wet zone. The call is often a duet, the male calling first followed almost immediately by the female. Thus, it is heard as a single call. They are known to breed from December to February. The nest is built from mosses, leaves etc. in to a bank, ledge, tree cavity, an open cup-shaped. 3-5 white eggs are laid.The Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler is classified as Near Threatened (NT) due to habitat destruction and degradation. The loss of its natural forest habitats, primarily due to deforestation, illegal logging, and agricultural expansion, poses a significant threat to its population. Conservation efforts, such as protecting Sri Lanka’s rainforests and promoting sustainable land-use practices, are vital to ensure the survival of this unique bird species. With its distinctive scimitar-shaped bill, lively nature, and important ecological role as an insect predator, the Ceylon Scimitar-Babbler is one of Sri Lanka’s remarkable and highly valued bird species.
